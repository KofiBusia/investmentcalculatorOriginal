What It Does

This guide walks you through the complete process of calculating a stock's intrinsic value using our suite of calculators. Follow these 10 steps to accurately value stocks using professional financial modeling techniques.

Pro Tip

Bookmark this page as a reference guide. Use the same time periods for all calculations and always verify data from reliable sources.

10-Step Calculation Process

1
Gather Historical Returns Data
  • Collect historical stock returns and market returns (e.g., monthly returns over 2-5 years)
  • Source this data from financial websites like Yahoo Finance or Bloomberg
  • For Ghana stocks, use GSE website or AFX Kwayisi
2
Calculate Beta
  • Open our Beta Calculator
  • Input historical stock returns and market returns
  • Click "Calculate" to obtain Beta value
  • Record Beta for use in Cost of Equity calculation
3
Gather Data for Cost of Equity
  • Risk-Free Rate: Current yield on long-term government bond (e.g., 10-year Ghana bond from Bank of Ghana)
  • Market Return: Historical average return of market index
  • Dividend per Share: Most recent annual dividend from financial statements
  • Current Stock Price: From stock exchange or financial website
  • Dividend Growth Rate: Based on historical growth or analyst projections
4
Calculate Cost of Equity
  • Open our Cost of Equity Calculator
  • For CAPM Method: Input Risk-Free Rate, Beta, and Market Return
  • For DDM Method: Input Dividend, Stock Price, and Growth Rate
  • Choose weights (e.g., 60% CAPM, 40% DDM)
  • Record the weighted average as Discount Rate
5
Gather Financial Data for FCFE
  • Net Income: From income statement
  • Net Capex: Capital Expenditures minus Depreciation
  • Change in Working Capital: From cash flow statement
  • Net Borrowing: New Debt minus Debt Repayment
  • Number of Shares Outstanding: From balance sheet
  • Source data from company's annual report
6
Calculate FCFE per Share
  • Open our FCFE Calculator
  • Input financial data
  • Click "Calculate" to get Free Cash Flow to Equity
  • Divide by Number of Shares to get FCFE per Share
7
Gather Other Inputs
  • Weighting Scenario: Conservative, Balanced, or Growth
  • Current Stock Price: From Step 3
  • Years for High Growth: 5-10 years based on industry trends
  • Growth Rates: Based on historical data
  • Dividends: From Step 3
  • EPS: From income statement
  • P/E Multiple: Industry average or historical P/E
8
Input Data into Multi-Method Calculator
  • Open our Multi-Method Valuation Calculator
  • Enter Weighting Scenario
  • Input Stock Price, Growth Years, and Rates
  • Provide Discount Rate (from Step 4)
  • Enter Dividends, FCFE per Share, and EPS
  • Input P/E Multiple and Projection Years
9
Calculate Intrinsic Value
  • Click "Calculate" in the Multi-Method Calculator
  • The calculator provides intrinsic value estimates using:
    • Dividend Discount Model (DDM)
    • Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)
    • P/E Multiple method
  • It also calculates a weighted average intrinsic value
10
Interpret Results
  • Compare weighted average intrinsic value to current stock price
  • If Intrinsic Value > Stock Price: Stock may be undervalued (buy opportunity)
  • If Intrinsic Value < Stock Price: Stock may be overvalued (consider selling)
  • Examine individual method results for consistency
  • Test different weighting scenarios for sensitivity analysis

Key Considerations

  • Use consistent time periods across all calculations
  • Verify all data points with reliable sources
  • For Ghana stocks, always use Ghana-specific data sources
  • Perform sensitivity analysis with different growth assumptions
  • Re-calculate periodically as new financial data becomes available

What It Does

This calculator estimates a stock's true value using a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model. The DCF model projects future cash flows and discounts them to their present value, helping you determine if a stock is undervalued or overvalued.

Pro Tip

Use 5 years of data for volatile companies and 3 years for stable ones. Always verify growth rates with historical data from reliable sources.

How to Use It

  1. Choose Years of Cash Flow

    Determine how many years of historical Free Cash Flow (FCF) data you want to include in your calculation. A range of 3 to 5 years is typically recommended depending on the company's stability.

    • For stable companies such as utility firms or well-established consumer goods companies, 3 years of FCF data is often sufficient due to their predictable cash flow patterns.
    • For volatile companies like tech startups or firms in emerging industries, opt for 5 years to account for fluctuations and obtain a more reliable average.
    • Tip: If you're uncertain about the company's stability, selecting 5 years provides a more conservative and comprehensive baseline.
  2. Enter FCF Amounts

    Input the Free Cash Flow (FCF) amounts for each of the selected years. FCF represents the cash a company generates after accounting for capital expenditures, available for distribution to shareholders.

    • Where to find FCF data: Obtain these figures from the company’s annual reports, financial statements, or trusted financial data platforms like Yahoo Finance, Bloomberg, or the company’s investor relations website.
    • Example: For a 3-year period from 2022 to 2024, you might enter 1,000,000 GHS for 2022, 1,200,000 GHS for 2023, and 1,500,000 GHS for 2024.
    • Note: Ensure all amounts are in the same currency to maintain consistency in your calculations.
  3. Select Growth Model

    Choose the growth model that best fits the company’s future cash flow projections, which will be used to estimate the stock’s intrinsic value.

    • Perpetual Growth Model: Appropriate for mature companies with stable and predictable growth rates. This model assumes cash flows grow at a constant rate indefinitely. Use this for firms in well-established industries with consistent performance.
    • Two-Stage Growth Model: Suitable for companies with an initial high-growth phase followed by a stable growth period. This model allows you to specify different growth rates for two distinct stages. Use this for firms in growth sectors like technology or biotech.
    • Tip: Research the company’s historical growth rates and industry trends to choose the most suitable model for accurate projections.

What It Does

This calculator estimates the intrinsic value of bank stocks using the Dividend Discount Model (DDM) for dividend-paying banks or the Residual Income Model (RIM) for growth-focused banks.

Pro Tip

Use DDM for banks with consistent dividend history and RIM for banks that reinvest earnings for growth. Always verify dividend patterns before choosing a model.

How to Use It

  1. Choose Valuation Model

    Select the valuation model that aligns with the bank’s financial profile and dividend policy to estimate its intrinsic value.

    • Dividend Discount Model (DDM): Ideal for banks that consistently pay dividends. This model calculates value based on the present value of expected future dividends. Use this if the bank has a reliable history of dividend payments.
    • Residual Income Model (RIM): Best for banks that reinvest earnings into growth rather than distributing dividends. This model uses book value and excess returns over the cost of equity. Use this for growth-oriented banks or those with irregular dividends.
    • Tip: Check the bank’s dividend history and strategic focus (e.g., growth vs. income) to select the most appropriate model.
  2. Set Forecast Period

    Specify the number of years for projecting the bank’s future financial performance, typically between 3 and 5 years.

    • 3 years: Choose this in uncertain or volatile market conditions where long-term forecasts are less reliable due to economic instability.
    • 5 years: Opt for this when market trends are stable and the bank’s financial performance is predictable over a longer period.
    • Tip: Consider current economic conditions and the bank’s historical performance to determine the most suitable forecast period.
  3. Enter CAPM Inputs

    Provide the components required for the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) to calculate the discount rate, which adjusts future cash flows to present value.

    • Risk-Free Rate: Use the current yield on 10-year government bonds as a baseline. For example, in Ghana, find this rate on the Bank of Ghana website or financial news outlets.
    • Expected Market Return: Estimate the average annual return of the broader stock market, typically ranging from 10-15%, adjusted based on historical data or local market conditions.
    • Beta: Input the stock’s volatility relative to the market, available from financial websites or calculated using our Beta Calculator.
    • Tip: Use up-to-date and region-specific data for each input to ensure accuracy in your discount rate calculation.

What It Does

This calculator computes a stock's beta, measuring its price volatility relative to the market. A beta > 1 indicates higher volatility, < 1 indicates lower volatility, and = 1 matches market movement.

Pro Tip

Use 12-24 months of data for accuracy. Ensure stock and market returns align in time periods (e.g., all monthly). Select the right market index for comparison.

How to Use It

  1. Gather Historical Returns

    Collect historical percentage returns for both the stock and the market index over consistent time periods (e.g., monthly or weekly) to calculate beta accurately.

    • For public stocks, access return data from financial platforms like Yahoo Finance, Google Finance, or Bloomberg.
    • For Ghana stocks, check the GSE website or AFX Kwayisi.
    • Tip: Use 12-24 months of data to balance accuracy and relevance, ensuring the periods align for both stock and market.
  2. Input Stock Returns

    Enter the stock’s historical returns as a series of comma-separated percentage values, reflecting the chosen time periods.

    • Example: For monthly returns over three months, you might input "5.2, -3.1, 4.7" (representing +5.2%, -3.1%, and +4.7%).
    • Note: Ensure the data is in percentage form and corresponds to the same intervals as the market returns.
  3. Input Market Returns

    Enter the market index’s historical returns as comma-separated percentage values, matching the stock return periods.

    • Example: For the same three months, input "4.1, -2.8, 5.0" if those are the market’s returns.
    • Tip: Select an appropriate market index (e.g., GSE All-Share Index for Ghana, S&P 500 for the U.S.) based on the stock’s market.
  4. Calculate Beta

    Click the "Calculate Beta" button to compute the stock’s beta value, which indicates its volatility relative to the market.

    • Result: The calculator will output a number (e.g., 1.2 means the stock is 20% more volatile than the market).
    • Tip: Verify the result aligns with the stock’s expected risk profile based on its industry and past performance.

What It Does

This calculator estimates the value of private companies or startups using three methods: Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) for stable businesses, Venture Capital Method for high-growth startups, and ARR Multiple for subscription-based firms.

Pro Tip

Use conservative growth rates and exit values. Match method to company type: DCF for stable firms, Venture Capital for startups, ARR for subscription models.

How to Use It

  • Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) - Best for firms with predictable cash flows

    Use this method for private companies with stable and predictable revenue streams to estimate their value based on discounted future cash flows.

    1. Input Free Cash Flows for 1–5 years: Enter projected FCF amounts for each year, sourced from financial forecasts or historical data if available.
    2. Provide risk-free rate, market return, and beta: Input these CAPM components to calculate the discount rate (e.g., risk-free rate from government bonds, market return estimate, beta based on industry peers or our Beta Calculator).
    3. Enter debt, equity, tax rate, and growth rate: Specify the company’s capital structure, tax rate, and expected long-term growth rate to finalize the valuation.
    4. Tip: Use conservative FCF projections and verify inputs with industry benchmarks for accuracy.
  • Venture Capital Method - Ideal for early-stage startups

    Apply this method to value high-growth startups by estimating their worth at exit, commonly used by venture capitalists.

    1. Specify expected exit value, target return, investment amount, exit timeline, and dilution factor: Input the anticipated sale value at exit (e.g., based on comparable exits), desired return rate (e.g., 10x), initial investment, years to exit, and ownership dilution percentage.
    2. Tip: Research recent startup exits in the same sector to set a realistic exit value and adjust for market conditions.
  • ARR Multiple - Suitable for subscription-based firms

    Use this method for companies with recurring revenue models, such as SaaS businesses, to estimate value based on revenue multiples.

    1. Input Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR) and ARR multiple: Enter the company’s current ARR (e.g., from financial reports) and an industry-standard ARR multiple (e.g., 5x–10x, depending on growth stage).
    2. Tip: Benchmark the ARR multiple against similar subscription businesses to ensure a market-relevant valuation.

What It Does

The Multi-Method Stock Valuation Calculator estimates a stock’s intrinsic value per share using three methods: Dividend Discount Model (DDM), Discounted Cash Flow (DCF), and Price-to-Earnings (P/E). It combines these with customizable weights to produce a weighted average intrinsic value, helping you determine if a stock is overvalued or undervalued against its market price. A sensitivity toggle offers three weighting scenarios—Conservative (dividend and cash flow focus), Balanced (cash flow emphasis), and Growth (market potential focus)—to suit different investment perspectives.

Pro Tip

Use accurate per-share data from reliable sources like gse.com.gh. Test all weighting scenarios to understand valuation range in volatile markets.

How to Use It

  1. Access the Calculator

    Visit the calculator online to begin the valuation process.

    • Go to the Multi-Method Valuation Calculator on our site.
  2. Choose Weighting Scenario

    Select a predefined weighting scenario to determine how much each valuation method (DDM, DCF, P/E) contributes to the final intrinsic value.

    • Conservative: Weights are 30% DDM Base, 20% DDM Sensitivity, 30% DCF, 20% P/E. Best for dividend-focused firms with stable cash flows.
    • Balanced: Weights are 20% DDM Base, 20% DDM Sensitivity, 40% DCF, 20% P/E. Ideal for companies with strong cash flows and moderate growth.
    • Growth: Weights are 20% DDM Base, 20% DDM Sensitivity, 30% DCF, 30% P/E. Suited for growth stocks with high market potential.
    • Tip: Choose based on the stock’s characteristics (e.g., Conservative for mature firms, Growth for tech startups).
  3. Enter General Parameters

    Input market and growth assumptions that apply across all valuation methods.

    • Current Stock Price (GHS): Find the latest price on gse.com.gh.
    • Number of Years for High Growth: Select 3–5 years based on the company’s growth phase.
    • High-Growth Rate (%): Estimate using historical data from annualreportsghana.com.
    • Terminal Growth Rate (%): Set to match long-term GDP growth, sourced from data.worldbank.org.
    • Discount Rate (%): Use the result from our Cost of Equity Calculator.
  4. Enter DDM Parameters

    Provide dividend-related inputs for the Dividend Discount Model.

    • Base Dividend per Share (GHS): Current dividend from gse.com.gh.
    • Sensitivity Dividend per Share (GHS): Enter a higher dividend scenario for sensitivity analysis.
    • Tip: Use historical dividend trends to inform your inputs.
  5. Enter DCF Parameters

    Input cash flow data for the Discounted Cash Flow model.

    • Current FCFE per Share (GHS): Calculate Free Cash Flow to Equity per share using our FCFE Calculator.
    • Tip: Ensure FCFE reflects the most recent fiscal year for accuracy.
  6. Enter P/E Parameters

    Provide earnings data for the Price-to-Earnings method.

    • Current EPS (GHS): Earnings per Share from annualreportsghana.com.
    • EPS Growth Rate (%): Base this on historical earnings growth.
    • P/E Multiple: Use an industry average from afx.kwayisi.org/gse/.
    • Projection Years for P/E: Choose 1–3 years for earnings projection.
  7. Calculate

    Click “Calculate” to process all inputs and generate the weighted average intrinsic value per share.

    • Result: Displays individual valuations from DDM, DCF, and P/E, plus the weighted average.
  8. Interpret Results

    Analyze the calculated intrinsic value against the current stock price to determine if the stock is overvalued (price > intrinsic value) or undervalued (price < intrinsic value).

    • Tip: Test different weighting scenarios to see how sensitive the valuation is to method emphasis.

What It Does

This calculator estimates the cost of equity, the return shareholders require, using CAPM (Cost = Risk-Free Rate + Beta × (Market Return - Risk-Free Rate)) and DDM (Cost = (Dividend per Share / Stock Price) + Growth Rate). It’s key for DCF valuations and WACC calculations.

Pro Tip

Use reliable sources like gse.com.gh. Adjust weights based on data reliability (e.g., higher CAPM weight if dividends are inconsistent).

How to Use It

  1. Access the Calculator

    Navigate to the Cost of Equity Calculator to start calculating the required return for shareholders.

    • Go to the Cost of Equity Calculator on our site.
  2. Gather Financial Data

    Collect the necessary financial data to compute the cost of equity using both CAPM and DDM methods.

    • CAPM Inputs: Obtain the Risk-Free Rate from bog.gov.gh, Beta from our Beta Calculator, and estimate Market Return (e.g., 12–15%) based on historical market performance.
    • DDM Inputs: Source Dividend per Share and Stock Price from gse.com.gh, and estimate Dividend Growth Rate (e.g., 5%) from historical dividend trends.
    • Weights: Assign weights to CAPM and DDM results (e.g., 60% CAPM, 40% DDM, totaling 100%) based on data reliability.
    • Tip: Use recent data to reflect current market conditions accurately.
  3. Enter Data

    Input the gathered values into the calculator’s form to compute the cost of equity.

    • Example: Enter Risk-Free Rate: 25%, Beta: 1.2, Market Return: 15%, Dividend: 0.12 GHS, Stock Price: 2.83 GHS, Growth Rate: 5%.
    • Note: Ensure all inputs are consistent (e.g., percentages for rates, same currency for monetary values).
  4. Calculate

    Click “Calculate” to generate the cost of equity based on the provided inputs.

    • Result: Outputs individual CAPM and DDM values (e.g., CAPM: 30%, DDM: 9.24%) and a weighted average (e.g., 21.7%).
    • Tip: Verify results align with industry norms for the stock’s sector.
  5. Use Results

    Apply the calculated cost of equity in financial models to assess investment viability.

    • Use the weighted cost as the discount rate in DCF valuations or as part of Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) calculations.
    • Tip: Adjust weights if one method’s data (e.g., dividends) is less reliable.

What It Does

This calculator computes Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE), the cash available to shareholders after expenses, investments, and debt obligations, using: FCFE = Net Income - Net Capex - Change in Working Capital + Net Borrowing. It’s key for DCF valuations.

Pro Tip

Use 5 years of data for stable trends, or 3 years in volatile markets. Verify financials from gse.com.gh.

How to Use It

  1. Access the Calculator

    Navigate to the FCFE Calculator to compute cash flow available to equity holders.

    • Go to the FCFE Calculator on our site.
  2. Gather Financial Data

    Collect financial data over a chosen period (typically 3–5 years) to calculate FCFE accurately.

    • For years (e.g., 2020–2024), gather Net Income, Net Capital Expenditures (Capex), Change in Working Capital, and Net Borrowing from sources like annualreportsghana.com.
    • Tip: Use 5 years for stable companies or 3 years in volatile markets to balance trend reliability and relevance.
  3. Enter Data

    Input the financial data for each year into the calculator to compute FCFE.

    • Example: For Year 1, enter Net Income: 1,000,000 GHS, Net Capex: 500,000 GHS, Change in Working Capital: 200,000 GHS, Net Borrowing: 300,000 GHS.
    • Note: Ensure all values are in the same currency and reflect the same fiscal period.
  4. Calculate

    Click “Calculate FCFE” to process the inputs and obtain the Free Cash Flow to Equity for each year.

    • Result: For Year 1 example, FCFE might be 600,000 GHS (1,000,000 - 500,000 - 200,000 + 300,000).
    • Tip: Check calculations against company financials to ensure accuracy.
  5. Use Results

    Utilize the calculated FCFE values in financial analysis or valuation models.

    • Apply FCFE in Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) models to estimate stock value or calculate FCFE per share by dividing by outstanding shares.
    • Tip: Use multiple years’ FCFE to identify trends and inform long-term investment decisions.

What It Does

The M&A Calculator analyzes the financial impact of mergers and acquisitions by calculating the accretion or dilution of Earnings Per Share (EPS). It combines the acquirer’s and target’s earnings, adjusts for synergies and new shares issued, and determines if the deal enhances or reduces shareholder value.

Pro Tip

Use current financial data from reliable sources like gse.com.gh. Estimate synergies conservatively to avoid over-optimism and verify share counts for accuracy.

How to Use It

  1. Access the Calculator

    Navigate to the M&A Calculator to evaluate the financial impact of a merger or acquisition.

    • Go to the M&A Calculator on our site.
  2. Gather Financial Data

    Collect financial data for both the acquiring and target companies to assess the deal’s impact on EPS.

    • Obtain EPS, shares outstanding, and expected synergies from financial statements or databases like annualreportsghana.com.
    • Tip: Use the most recent annual or quarterly reports for up-to-date figures.
  3. Enter Input Values

    Input the required financial metrics for both companies and the deal structure into the calculator.

    • Acquirer EPS: Pre-deal Earnings Per Share of the acquiring company (Net Income ÷ Shares Outstanding).
    • Acquirer Shares Outstanding: Total shares issued by the acquirer before the merger.
    • Target EPS: Pre-deal EPS of the target company (Net Income ÷ Shares Outstanding).
    • Target Shares Outstanding: Total shares issued by the target before the merger.
    • New Shares Issued in Deal: Number of new shares the acquirer will issue to finance or complete the merger.
    • Expected Annual Synergy Value (GHS): Estimated annual earnings boost from cost savings or revenue increases due to the merger.
    • Tip: Estimate synergies conservatively to reflect realistic post-merger benefits.
  4. Calculate

    Click the “Calculate” button to process the inputs and determine the deal’s effect on EPS.

    • Result: Outputs combined earnings, total shares post-deal, and pro-forma EPS.
  5. Interpret Results

    Analyze the results to assess whether the merger or acquisition is accretive or dilutive to EPS.

    • Accretive: If pro-forma EPS exceeds the acquirer’s pre-deal EPS, the deal increases shareholder value.
    • Dilutive: If pro-forma EPS is lower than the acquirer’s pre-deal EPS, the deal reduces shareholder value.
    • Tip: Consider synergies and deal structure adjustments if the result is dilutive to explore value-enhancing options.
